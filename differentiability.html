Differentiability & Why Deep Learning Needs It

The complete explanation — from first principles to backpropagation — so you never have to think about this again

1. Slope, tangent lines, and derivatives — the three views of the same thing

What is slope?

Slope is the simplest idea here. Take any straight line. Pick two points on it. Slope is: how much the line goes up or down ÷ how much it goes sideways.

If a line goes from point (1, 2) to point (4, 8), it went up by 6 (from 2 to 8) and sideways by 3 (from 1 to 4). Slope = 6/3 = 2. Meaning: for every 1 unit you move right, the line goes up by 2 units.

A positive slope means the line goes uphill (left to right). A negative slope means downhill. A slope of zero means the line is perfectly flat — horizontal.

For a straight line, the slope is the same everywhere. That's what makes it a straight line. Pick any two points and you'll get the same number.

The problem with curves

A curve is not a straight line. Its steepness changes from point to point. Think of a hill — at the bottom it's gentle, near the top it's steep, at the very top it's flat again. The slope is different at every location.

So how do you define "slope" at a single point on a curve? You can't use the two-points formula because a single point doesn't give you a direction. This is where the tangent line comes in.

What is a tangent line?

Take a curve. Pick a point on it. Now imagine laying a straight ruler against the curve at that exact point, so the ruler just barely touches the curve and matches its direction at that spot. That ruler is the tangent line.

More precisely: take your point P, and take another point Q somewhere else on the curve. Draw the straight line through P and Q — this is called a secant line. Now slide Q closer and closer to P along the curve. As Q approaches P, the secant line rotates and settles into a limiting position. That limiting line is the tangent.

The tangent is the straight line that best approximates the curve at that one specific point. If you zoomed in really close on the curve at that point, the curve and the tangent would become indistinguishable — they'd look like the same line.

How slope, tangent, and derivative are connected

The relationship is: the derivative at a point = the slope of the tangent line at that point.

All three concepts collapse into the same number, just viewed differently:

ViewWhat it isHow you think about it
GeometricTangent lineA picture — the straight line that touches the curve and matches its direction
AlgebraicSlope of that tangentA number — rise over run of the tangent line
CalculusDerivativeA formula — computes that same number using the limit definition

So when someone says "the derivative of f(x) = x² at x = 3 is 6," they're saying three equivalent things: (a) the tangent line at x = 3 has slope 6, (b) the curve is going up at a rate of 6 units of output per 1 unit of input at that point, and (c) if you nudge x from 3 by a tiny amount ε, the output changes by approximately 6ε.

Why do we care about the tangent specifically? Because it converts a question about a curve (hard) into a question about a straight line (easy). "How steep is this curve here?" is hard to answer directly. But "what's the slope of this straight line?" is trivial — rise over run. The tangent is the bridge.

And in deep learning, this is everything: the derivative tells you "if I increase this weight by a tiny amount, the loss goes up by [this much]." That's the only information gradient descent needs.

The formal definition of the derivative

Now that we have the intuition, here's the formal version. For a function f(x), the derivative at point x₀ is:

f ′(x₀)  =  limh→0  f(x₀ + h) − f(x₀)h

Read this carefully:

  • The numerator f(x₀ + h) − f(x₀) is "how much did the output change" when we nudged the input by h.
  • The denominator h is "how much did the input change."
  • Their ratio is "output change per unit of input change" — the slope.
  • The limit h → 0 makes this the instantaneous rate of change, not an average.

Worked example: derivative of f(x) = x² at x = 3

f ′(3)  =  limh→0  (3 + h)² − 3²h plug in x₀ = 3
=  limh→0  9 + 6h + h² − 9h expand (3+h)² = 9 + 6h + h²
=  limh→0  6h + h²h the 9's cancel
=  limh→0  (6 + h) divide each term by h
=  6 as h → 0, we're left with 6

So at x = 3, the slope is 6. Let's verify: if you nudge x from 3 to 3.001, the output changes by approximately 6 × 0.001 = 0.006. Check: 3.001² = 9.006001. The actual change is 0.006001 — almost exactly what the derivative predicted.

In general, the derivative of x² is 2x. At x = 3, that's 2(3) = 6. At x = −2, it's 2(−2) = −4. The negative sign means: if you increase x, the output decreases. The derivative encodes both magnitude (how much) and direction (which way).

2. What does "differentiable" mean?

A function is differentiable at a point if the derivative exists there — the limit above actually converges to a single, finite number.

A function is differentiable (without qualification) if it's differentiable at every point in its domain.

When is a function not differentiable? The precise rule is: the derivative at a point exists only if the slope approaching that point from the left and the slope approaching from the right both exist and are equal. If they disagree, or if either doesn't exist, the derivative is undefined.

This gives us three cases where differentiability fails:

Case 1: Sharp corners (kinks)

At a sharp corner, the slope from the left and the slope from the right converge to different values. There's no single tangent line. The classic example is f(x) = |x| at x = 0.

As you approach x = 0 from the right (x = 0.1, 0.01, 0.001...), the slope is always +1. As you approach from the left (x = −0.1, −0.01, −0.001...), the slope is always −1. Both limits exist individually, but they disagree: +1 ≠ −1. So the derivative at x = 0 doesn't exist.

Common misconception: "The bottom of the V is a minimum, so the tangent should be horizontal (slope = 0) there." This is wrong. A horizontal tangent means "the function is momentarily flat — a tiny nudge barely changes the output." But at |x| at x = 0, the function is not flat at all. Nudge x from 0 to 0.001 and |x| jumps to 0.001 — full speed, slope = +1. Nudge to −0.001 and |x| also jumps to 0.001 — slope = −1 from that side. The function is changing at full speed in both directions, just with contradictory slopes that no single line can capture. That's why the derivative is undefined, not zero.

Compare with a smooth minimum: at f(x) = x² at x = 0, the curve gently flattens out. As you approach from the right (slopes: 0.2, 0.02, 0.002...), the slope approaches 0. From the left (slopes: −0.2, −0.02, −0.002...), also approaches 0. Both sides agree on 0. The derivative exists and equals 0. The tangent is genuinely horizontal because the curve is genuinely flat at that instant.

The key distinction: smooth minimum (like x² at 0) → tangent exists, it's horizontal, derivative = 0. Sharp minimum (like |x| at 0) → no tangent exists, derivative is undefined.

Case 2: Discontinuities (jumps)

If the function jumps from one value to another, the derivative is undefined at the jump. Example: the step function (0 for x < 0, 1 for x ≥ 0). At x = 0, the output jumps by 1 over zero distance. The slope from the left is 0 (flat line), the slope from the right is also 0 (flat line), but the function itself isn't even continuous — it skips a value — so the limit in the derivative definition doesn't exist at all.

Case 3: Vertical tangents / cusps

If the curve goes vertical at a point, the slope is infinite. Example: f(x) = x1/3 at x = 0. The left and right slopes both approach infinity, so the derivative doesn't exist as a finite number.

Key intuition: differentiable means "locally linear." If you zoom in close enough on a differentiable point, the curve looks like a straight line. At a non-differentiable point, no matter how much you zoom in, you still see the kink. The function doesn't have a single "direction" there.

3. Why deep learning cares: gradient descent

Training a neural network is an optimization problem. You have a loss function (a measure of how wrong the model is), and you want to find the weight values that make it as small as possible. A typical network has millions of weights. You can't try all combinations. You need a directed strategy.

That strategy is gradient descent:

1. Compute the derivative of the loss with respect to each weight. This tells you: "if I increase this weight slightly, will the loss go up or down, and by how much?"

2. Nudge each weight in the direction that makes the loss smaller.

3. Repeat millions of times.

The update rule:

wnew  =  wold  −  η  ·  ∂ ℒ∂ w

Where each symbol means:

SymbolNameWhat it is
wWeightA single learnable parameter in the network
η (eta)Learning rateA scalar controlling step size. Too big → overshoot. Too small → crawl.
∂ℒ∂wGradientPartial derivative of the loss w.r.t. this weight. "How much does the loss change if I nudge this weight?"
− signDirectionThe derivative points uphill (steepest ascent). We want to go downhill, so we subtract.

The blindfolded hiker analogy

Imagine you're blindfolded on a hilly landscape. You can't see, but you can feel the slope under your feet. If the ground tilts left, you step left. If it tilts right, you step right. The steeper the tilt, the bigger your step. Eventually the ground feels flat — you've found a valley.

The derivative is the "slope under your feet." Without it, you're standing still with zero information about which direction leads downhill. No derivative = no direction = no learning.

What if the gradient is always zero? Then the update becomes wnew = wold − η × 0 = wold. The weight never changes. It's frozen forever. This is exactly what happens when a non-differentiable or flat function sits in the network.

4. From one weight to millions: backpropagation and the chain rule

The previous section showed gradient descent for one weight. A real network has millions of weights across many layers. How do you compute the derivative of the loss with respect to a weight buried deep in layer 1, when the loss is computed many layers later?

The answer is backpropagation, which is just the chain rule from calculus applied systematically.

What is the chain rule?

The chain rule says: if you compose two functions — f(g(x)) — the derivative of the whole thing is the product of the individual derivatives:

ddx f(g(x))  =  f ′(g(x))  ·  g′(x)

In words: "the rate of change of the whole chain = rate of change of the outer function × rate of change of the inner function."

Worked example of the chain rule

Let f(u) = u² and g(x) = 3x + 1. Then the composition is f(g(x)) = (3x + 1)².

ddx (3x + 1)²  =  f ′(g(x))  ·  g′(x) apply chain rule
=  2 · (3x + 1)  ·  3 f′(u) = 2u, evaluated at u = g(x);   g′(x) = 3
=  6(3x + 1) simplify

Verification by expanding first:

(3x + 1)²  =  9x² + 6x + 1 expand the square
ddx (9x² + 6x + 1)  =  18x + 6  =  6(3x + 1) same answer ✓

The chain rule gave us the correct derivative without having to expand first. This matters because in a neural network, you can't expand — the compositions are too deep and too complex. The chain rule is the only practical way.

The chain rule extends to any depth

For three nested functions f(g(h(x))):

ddx f(g(h(x)))  =  f ′(g(h(x)))  ·  g′(h(x))  ·  h′(x)

You just keep multiplying. Each function contributes its own local derivative, evaluated at the appropriate input. For n nested functions, it's a product of n terms.

A neural network IS a chain of functions

A neural network with 3 layers computes a chain of transformations:

Each layer applies a function: a linear transformation (matrix multiply with weights) followed by a nonlinear activation. The loss ℒ is computed by comparing the final output to the target.

To update the weights w₁ in Layer 1, we need ∂ℒ∂w₁. By the chain rule:

∂ ℒ∂ w₁  =  ∂ ℒ∂ L₃  ·  ∂ L₃∂ L₂  ·  ∂ L₂∂ L₁  ·  ∂ L₁∂ w₁

Each fraction is a local derivative: "how much does this layer's output change when its input changes?" Backpropagation computes these layer-by-layer, from output back to input, and multiplies them together.

Why one broken link kills the entire chain

The chain rule is a product. Anything multiplied by zero is zero. So:

ScenarioTerm valuesProductEffect
All healthy0.8 × 0.8 × 0.8 × 0.80.4096Gradient flows. Learning works.
One zero0.8 × 0.8 × 0 × 0.80Gradient dead. w₁ can never learn.

The zero case is directly caused by non-differentiability. If any layer has a function whose derivative is zero (like a step function), it contributes a zero to the product, and the entire gradient dies. All weights in earlier layers are frozen.

The vanishing case happens with functions like sigmoid, whose derivative maxes out at 0.25. In a 50-layer network: 0.25⁵⁰ ≈ 10⁻³⁰. Technically nonzero, but useless.

The exploding case is the opposite: derivatives > 1 compound exponentially. In a 50-layer network: 1.5⁵⁰ ≈ 6.4 × 10⁸. Wild weight updates, unstable training.

5. Activation functions: the make-or-break layer

Every layer in a neural network applies: (1) a linear transformation (matrix multiply), then (2) a nonlinear activation function. Without the activation, stacking layers is pointless — a composition of linear functions is still linear, and the network can only learn linear relationships.

The activation function's derivative determines whether gradients flow or die. This makes it one of the most consequential design decisions in deep learning.

Sigmoid

σ(x)  =  11 + e−x squishes any input to (0, 1)
σ′(x)  =  σ(x) · (1 − σ(x)) max value is 0.25, at x = 0

Smooth and differentiable everywhere. But the derivative maxes out at 0.25 and drops to near-zero for large |x|. In a 10-layer network: 0.25¹⁰ ≈ 10⁻⁶. In a 50-layer network: effectively zero. This is the vanishing gradient problem. Deep sigmoid networks are nearly impossible to train. Used today only in output layers (binary classification) and gating mechanisms (LSTMs).

Step function

step(x)  =  1  if  x ≥ 0,    0  if  x < 0
step′(x)  =  0    everywhere derivative is flat zero

The worst case. Not differentiable at x = 0 (discontinuity), and derivative is exactly zero everywhere else. The gradient is always zero. No weight can ever learn through a step function. This is why the original Perceptron (hard threshold) couldn't be trained with backpropagation. The invention of backpropagation required switching to smooth activations.

ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit)

ReLU(x)  =  max(0, x)
ReLU′(x)  =  1  if  x > 0,    0  if  x ≤ 0 constant 1 for positive inputs!

The breakthrough that enabled deep learning (AlexNet, 2012). For positive inputs, the derivative is a constant 1 — gradients pass through with zero attenuation. In a 100-layer network with all-positive activations: 1¹⁰⁰ = 1. The gradient is perfectly preserved. No vanishing.

The catch: for negative inputs, the derivative is zero. A neuron with negative input is "dead" — it contributes zero gradient. If a neuron always receives negative inputs (bad initialization, large negative biases), it dies permanently. This is the dying ReLU problem. In practice it's tolerable because enough neurons stay positive.

ReLU is technically not differentiable at x = 0 (it has a kink), but in practice we define the derivative as 0 there. The probability of landing exactly at 0 is negligible.

Leaky ReLU

LeakyReLU(x)  =  x  if  x ≥ 0,    αx  if  x < 0 typically α = 0.01
LeakyReLU′(x)  =  1  if  x ≥ 0,    α  if  x < 0 never zero!

Fixes the dying neuron problem. The derivative is never zero: 1 for positive inputs, α (small but nonzero) for negative inputs. Every neuron always gets some gradient signal. Modern variants: Parametric ReLU (α is learnable), ELU, GELU (used in GPT/BERT), SiLU/Swish — all designed to keep the gradient nonzero everywhere.

6. Partial derivatives and the gradient vector

So far we've dealt with derivatives of functions of one variable. But a neural network has millions of weights. The loss is a function of all of them: ℒ(w₁, w₂, ..., wn).

A partial derivative is the derivative with respect to one variable while holding all others constant:

∂ ℒ∂ wk  =  limh→0  ℒ(w₁, ..., wk+h, ..., wn) − ℒ(w₁, ..., wk, ..., wn)h

"If I change just this one weight by a tiny amount, and freeze everything else, how much does the loss change?"

The collection of all partial derivatives is the gradient vector:

∇w ℒ  =  ( ∂ℒ∂w₁ ,  ∂ℒ∂w₂ ,  ... ,  ∂ℒ∂wn )

This vector points in the direction of steepest ascent in weight-space. Gradient descent steps in the opposite direction:

wnew  =  wold  −  η  ·  ∇w ℒ updates ALL weights at once

Each weight gets its own nudge, proportional to its own partial derivative. Weights that strongly affect the loss get larger updates. Weights that barely matter get tiny updates. The gradient gives each weight exactly the right amount of correction.

7. The full picture: forward pass → backward pass → update

Step 1: Forward pass
Input data flows through the network layer by layer. Each layer does: (a) linear transform (matrix multiply with weights + bias), (b) nonlinear activation. The final output is compared to the target using a loss function, producing a single scalar loss value ℒ. Every intermediate value is saved in memory — we'll need them for the backward pass.
Step 2: Backward pass (backpropagation)
Starting from the loss, we compute ∂ℒ/∂(output of last layer). Then we propagate this backward through each layer using the chain rule. At each layer, we compute the local derivative (how much this layer's output changes per unit change in its input), multiply it into the running product, and also compute the derivative with respect to this layer's weights. By the end, every weight in the network has its own gradient.
Step 3: Weight update
Every weight is updated simultaneously: wnew = wold − η · gradient. The weights shift in the direction that reduces the loss. Then we go back to Step 1 with the next batch of training data, and repeat.
What makes this all possible
Differentiability. Every single operation in the forward pass — every matrix multiply, every activation function, the loss function itself — must be differentiable. If any one of them isn't, the chain rule breaks at that point and no gradient can flow past it. All weights before that point are frozen. The entire architecture of modern neural networks is constrained by this single requirement: the gradient must flow, unbroken, from the loss all the way back to every weight.

8. Tricks for when you need non-differentiable operations

Sometimes you genuinely need a discrete, non-differentiable operation — like choosing one option out of many, or quantizing a value to integers. Here are the standard workarounds:

Straight-through estimator (STE)
Use the non-differentiable function in the forward pass, but pretend the derivative is 1 in the backward pass. The gradient just passes through as if the function were the identity. Crude but effective. Used in binary neural networks and quantization-aware training.
Gumbel-softmax (concrete relaxation)
Replace a hard categorical choice (argmax) with a soft, differentiable approximation. Instead of picking one option, compute a weighted average where one option dominates. A temperature parameter controls how "hard" the selection is — high temperature gives a soft average, low temperature approximates argmax. Used in VAEs with discrete latent variables.
Softmax gating
Make the hard choice via argmax (non-differentiable), but multiply the output by the softmax probability (differentiable). The choice doesn't get gradients, but the confidence in the choice does. The router learns by adjusting how confident it is, not by changing which option is selected. Used in Mixture-of-Experts models (Switch Transformer, Mixtral).
REINFORCE / policy gradient
Treat the non-differentiable operation as a policy in a reinforcement learning setup. Sample actions from the probability distribution, observe the reward (loss), and update the probabilities using the REINFORCE gradient estimator. Doesn't require differentiability at all, but has high variance and is harder to train. Used in neural architecture search, discrete optimization.
Residual connections (skip connections)
Not a fix for non-differentiability, but for vanishing gradients. Add the input directly to the output: y = f(x) + x. During backprop, the gradient flows through both f and the identity shortcut. Even if f's gradient vanishes, the identity provides a gradient of exactly 1. This is why ResNets can train 100+ layers. Nearly all modern architectures use this, including every Transformer.

9. The five things to remember forever

1. A derivative measures rate of change
"If I nudge the input, how much does the output move, and in which direction?" Positive derivative → output increases. Negative → output decreases. Zero → output doesn't change. It's the slope of the tangent line.
2. Differentiable means the derivative exists
Smooth curves are differentiable. Sharp corners, jumps, and discontinuities are not. At a non-differentiable point, the function has no single "direction" — and the gradient is undefined.
3. Gradient descent needs the derivative as a compass
The derivative tells each weight which direction to move to reduce the loss, and by how much. No derivative → the weight is frozen. It has zero information about how to improve.
4. The chain rule multiplies derivatives across layers
Backpropagation = the chain rule applied backward through the network. The gradient for early-layer weights is the product of all later layers' local derivatives. One zero → dead. Many small values → vanishing. Many large values → exploding.
5. Every design choice in deep learning serves the gradient
Activation functions (ReLU over sigmoid), residual connections, careful initialization, batch normalization, gradient clipping, learning rate schedules — all exist for one reason: to keep the gradient flowing from the loss back to every weight without dying, vanishing, or exploding. The entire field is an engineering effort to keep the chain rule product healthy.
